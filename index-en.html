What is Hepatitis B?

Hepatitis B is the most common serious liver infection in the world. It is caused by the hepatitis B virus, which attacks and damages the liver. Each year, up to one million people die from hepatitis B, despite it being preventable and treatable.

Hepatitis B is transmitted through direct contact with infected blood or certain bodily fluids. The virus is most often transmitted from an infected pregnant woman to her baby during childbirth, due to blood exchange between the mother and her baby.

Hepatitis B is a "silent epidemic" because most people with recent or chronic infections show no symptoms. They can therefore unknowingly transmit the virus to others and perpetuate the silent spread of hepatitis B.

The good news is that hepatitis B is preventable and treatable. A simple blood test can diagnose hepatitis B infection. Testing is the only way to know for sure if you are infected.

Symptoms of Hepatitis B

These symptoms include:

  • Abdominal Pain
  • Chronic fatigue
  • Yellowing of the eyes (jaundice)
  • Dark urine
  • Intestinal problems
  • Abdominal swelling
  • Appearance of liver spots

In some infected people, the hepatitis B virus can remain silent for many years. This is called an inactive or dormant virus: it is present in the body but causes no apparent symptoms.

Prevention and Advice

1. 💉 Vaccination

  • The hepatitis B vaccine is the best prevention.
  • Recommended from birth or at any age if not vaccinated.
  • It is safe and highly effective (over 95% protection).

2. ⚠️ Sexual Precautions

  • Always use condoms with new partners.
  • Get tested regularly if you are sexually active with multiple partners.

3. 🩸 Avoid contact with blood

  • Never share needles, syringes, razors, nail clippers, or toothbrushes.
  • Check the hygiene and sterilization of equipment at tattoo artists, piercers, and nail salons.

4. 🏥 Screening

  • If you are at risk, get tested.
  • This helps detect the infection even if you are not experiencing symptoms and protects others.

5. 👶 Prevention in Newborns

If the mother is infected, it is urgent to vaccinate the baby at birth and administer a specific immunoglobulin to prevent transmission.
